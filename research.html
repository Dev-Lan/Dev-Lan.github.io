2015

For the summer of 2015 to present I worked with Professor Daniel Keefe at the University of Minnesota Interactive Visualization Lab. Taking inspiration from my work with multi-agent navigation I decided to develop an open source tool in C++ for exploring multivariate trajectory data through visualization. To me the most interesting aspect of the tool is the feature that allows you to expand time into a spatial dimension. But it also uses some common visualization techniques such as linking and brushing, parallel coordinate plots and uniform color maps. The project is still in development, but a first release of the source code and binary should be out soon.

For the summer of 2014 I was fortunate enough to have the opportunity to perform research abroad at Inria Rennes under Julien Pettr√©. This ended up being an educational experience for me in many different domains of life. In terms of my academic pursuits the most prominent event was the chance to participate in the end process of publication. Optimization-based computation of locomotion trajectories for crowd patches became the first paper that I co-authored. I discovered a lot about the publication process while writing, editing and created diagrams for the paper. Most fondly I remember the late nights creating and editing a final video for our paper. As it turns out, the 3D visualization I hacked together for that video became an early inspiration for my multivariate trajectory visualization work. The rest of the summer I spent developing algorithms in C++ that expanded on those in the paper. I appreciate the technical practice, as well as the team communication skills I got from working within a larger project. However my favorite aspect of this job was ruminating on manipulating space-time paths both at and after work.

2013

The summer after my freshman year of college I was eager to begin my first experience with academic research. Professor Stephen J. Guy was gracious enough to take a green programmer such as myself into his Applied Motion Lab. There I first learned the basics of representing motion programatically and utilized them by implementing a Python version of A Predictive Collision Avoidance Model for Pedestrian Simulation. I spent the remainder of my time developing code in Python that would read in a crowd simulation and use Mitsuba to render a high quality video of the crowd. I thoroughly enjoyed creating a tool that people in the lab could use. Furthermore reading through the documentation of Mitsuba was my first foray into the world of computer graphics.
